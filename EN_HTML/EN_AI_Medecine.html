The use of AI in medicine is a revolution that is close.

AI surrounds us but remains complex in its use. To summarize, AI refers to artificial intelligence. It exists in two forms: weak AI, which already surrounds us and allows for the execution of more or less complex lists of instructions called algorithms, acting as if it is intelligent because it was designed to do so but is not truly intelligent, such as voice assistants; and strong AI, which does not yet exist today but whose goal is to be able to learn on its own, improve, and become autonomous. These artificial intelligences, through their complexity and the methods they have (machine learning), will greatly aid medicine.

Currently, AI is attempting to replicate human medical activities, aiming to make doctors' tasks easier such as analyzing radio images to detect tumors on a scan. In the future, this technology is expected to be used in predicting a disease and its evolution and in recommending personalized treatment and in helping with decision-making, allowing doctors to target a patient's potential diseases as accurately as possible.

Artificial intelligence is making great technological advances in medicine, but it is still somewhat in its infancy because the use of computer programs that are related to or have an impact on humans is highly regulated, and anything that touches it requires very complex procedures that can take a long time to be approved for public use. AI is already beginning to revolutionize the world in other fields such as the creation of synthesized images, using AI like Midjournée, a very powerful tool but it poses moral and human problems because it goes against human principles. This could also be the case for artificial intelligences in the field of medicine: can strong AI be only intended to save us? For now, weak computer programs are preferred.
